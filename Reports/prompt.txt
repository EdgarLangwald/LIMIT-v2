  LIMIT v1:

  Terminology:

  - document: The thing that makes one embedding vector. “John Smith likes apples, olive oil, horses, …”
  - m: number of items per document
  - n: number of documents

  Experiments:

  - Increase-m: Fix some n (e.g. 40). Then, repeatedly attribute m = 1, …, 46 many items to every document. Each item appears only
  once throughout the ENTIRE dataset - no combinatorial augmentation.
  - Steiner: Fix m = 3. Create as many documents as possible whilst satisfying the condition, that every pair of items appears in
  exactly one document.
  - Preference: Some experimentation with disliking items as well

  Results and takeaways:

  - promptriever drastically outperforms all models, and Snowflake outperforms other small models like Qwen0.6b
  - Increase_m shows that models are incapable of embedding 50 items to begin with -> The original experiment of the LIMIT paper
  doesn't recognize this flaw and calls it a geometric problem.
  - promptriever demonstrates that even with 570k very similarly structured docs, it's possible to have racall@5 at over 99%

  New question:

  - How much does dataset variability affect relative model performance? I.e. can we use the simple synthetic cases to estimate
  performance on more realistic settings?

  LIMIT v2:

  - Documents are now entire profiles with realistic sentences. “John Smith was born on … As a child, he refused…. bla bla”
  - m now refers to number of sentences per doc. n is still number of docs.
  - Dataset is now split into n distractor documents and custom query targets.
  - Distractor docs are created as follows:
      - Sample a name, date of birth and job to create a standard introduction
      - Sample 4 categories (with weighting by frequency value)
      - Sample 1 template sentence and one item per category, and fill in the template (name, nouns, item)
  - Target docs and queries are created as follows:
      - step 1 from above
      - Sample 3 categories instead of 4
      - Sample one item per category
      - Call a Haiku agent to do the following:
          - write a broad and a specific query, asking about the person in relation to the item and
          - pick two templates per category that match as answer to that query
          - (review its answer)
          - return the query and the two template sentence numbers
      - Create 8 profiles - one for each combination of the two provided templates
      - Pad each profile with another sentence, each sampled at random from any other category

  Experiments:

  - By hand: embed the distractor set and probe retrieval manually by embedding the raw query and returning the top 10 set, as well
  of the rank of any specified doc
  note: I did the probing BEFORE deciding on parameters for main experiment and target creation
  - MAIN: Testing the recall of the target profiles with increasing numbers of distractor pool size.

  Clarification: Third experiment is bad with regards to the conclusion they want to make. Not bad per se.

  The paper can be split into three main sections.

  - In order to return all k-subsets for n docs, we need at least d = lower_bound(k, n) many dimensions.
  → the lower bound is entirely dependent on the margin of choice, and gamma 0.1 seems somewhat arbitrary
  - Directly optimizing embeddings and queries against loss that scores retrieval of all 2-subsets gives a minimum dimension needed
  for n docs to get loss to 0. Genuinly interesting result!
  → the experiment only goes till n =~ 620, requiring < 50 dims. Extrapolation is done with a cubic polynomial, but with no
  explanation of why. Why not fit higher or lower degree polynomials, or other functions entirely? The results don’t automatically
  translate to higher n.
  → If we believe their lower bound, d=4096 still gets us 250 million embedding vectors, for whom we can retrieve ALL 2 choose 250
  million possible 2-subsets perfectly. My opinion: enough in practice
  - Third experiment tests model capacity to return all possible pairs of document for n=46 docs, with and without distractor pool
  respectively. Indeed, embedding models have trouble retrieving the two relevant docs.
  → My experiment shows that with just k=1 and without distractors, models struggle to return the relevant doc. We therefore have no
  good baseline to compare the difficulty of retrieving 2-subsets against.
  → They truncated the embeddings to reduce dimension below what models really output. There’s no way to tell how much retrieval
  quality suffers from truncation vs. lower dimensionality.
  → They acknowledge themselves that promptriever wins "perhaps because their training allows them to use more of their embedding
  space.”, implying they themselves think that models underutilize the embedding space they have instead of dimension being a
  “fundamental limit”
  → from 1024 onward, more dimensions don’t really help the models in the experiment with distractors.
